Spices, herbs & foods that actually work.

A pragmatic field guide to the household plants, spices and foods with credible peer-reviewed evidence behind them — ginger, lemongrass, cloves, turmeric, garlic, hibiscus, moringa, leafy greens, nuts and more. The aim is to separate established benefits from folklore, and to give practical preparation guidance. None of this replaces a doctor for serious illness.

An honest framing Foods and herbs can support health — they cannot reliably cure serious disease. The remedies below have credible evidence for specific, often modest effects. They are not substitutes for vaccinations, prescribed medications, cancer treatment, or evaluation of serious symptoms. Some interact with medications (garlic, ginger, turmeric with anticoagulants; grapefruit with many drugs); discuss any regular medicinal use with your clinician.

The pantry

Putting it together — practical combinations

For nausea

Sliced fresh ginger (2 cm) simmered in water for 10 min, sweetened lightly with honey. 1–2 cups/day. Effective for motion sickness, morning sickness, post-operative nausea.

For mild blood pressure elevation

Hibiscus tea (2 cups daily, unsweetened) + 1 fresh garlic clove crushed and rested 10 min before eating + 2–3 cups oily-fish weekly + brisk walking 30 min/day. Reasonable to try before medication if BP is borderline (130–139/80–89), with your clinician.

For common cold and sore throat

Warm lemon and honey (>1 year old only), with grated ginger and a pinch of cayenne. Soothes throat; honey is a modest cough suppressant.

For mild joint stiffness

Turmeric milk: ½ tsp turmeric, ½ tsp grated ginger, pinch of black pepper, in warm milk (or plant milk) at bedtime. Curcumin has modest evidence for osteoarthritis pain.

For IBS or bloating

Enteric-coated peppermint oil 0.2 mL three times daily before meals has the strongest evidence. Reduce ultra-processed food and FODMAP triggers. Discuss with a gastroenterologist if symptoms persist.

For everyday cardio-protection

30 g of nuts daily (mixed: walnuts, almonds, groundnuts — discard mouldy), one tablespoon ground flaxseed, fresh garlic and ginger in cooking, leafy greens daily, hibiscus 2× weekly. Cumulative effects matter more than any single ingredient.

What does not work (despite popular claims)

Caution: avoid
  • "Liver detoxes" and "kidney cleanses" — the liver and kidneys are continuous detoxifiers; some traditional formulas contain nephrotoxic aristolochic acid (IARC Group 1 carcinogen).
  • High-dose green tea extract supplements — multiple case reports of acute liver injury. Drinking green tea is fine; concentrated extracts are not.
  • Colloidal silver — causes irreversible argyria (blue-grey skin discolouration) and has no proven therapeutic benefit.
  • Whole-leaf aloe vera taken orally — linked to colorectal tumours in animal studies (US NTP). Topical inner-gel use is fine.
  • "Cancer-curing" herbal preparations — none have credible RCT evidence. They delay effective treatment.
  • Unlicensed weight-loss "naturals" — frequently adulterated with stimulants, diuretics, or hidden pharmaceuticals.
How to evaluate any health claim Look for: peer-reviewed sources (PubMed, Cochrane, major medical journals); randomised controlled trials with placebo arms; effect sizes (not just "significant" but "by how much"); systematic reviews and meta-analyses; consistency across studies; biological plausibility. Be sceptical of single small studies, anecdotes, "ancient wisdom" alone, and anything that promises a cure for a serious disease.
